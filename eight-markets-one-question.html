Eight Markets, One Question

Leading multi-phase global market research commissioned as the foundational research for a global marketing strategy — surveying surgeons, interviewing proceduralists and procurement stakeholders, landscape assessment with peer-reviewed literature review, and stimuli-based protocol testing. Exploring the ground for scaling a new surgical procedure globally — a study designed around triangulation that taught me more about research discipline than any single project before it.

Outcome

Turned an ambiguous global adoption question into a structured, triangulated evidence base. Grounding a commercial strategy in evidence rather than assumption.

The Challenge

The client needed to understand whether a new interventional surgical procedure could work globally — not in theory, but in the real world. Across different clinical specialties, hospital workflows, procurement systems, imaging capabilities, and regional constraints.

The surface question was about tools: which needles, which imaging modalities, which access routes. The real question was harder: Can this procedure be adopted, globally, by the clinicians who would perform it?

That question required knowing what existed, what was feasible, and where the gaps were — across eight geographies: the US, France, Germany, Spain, Brazil, China, Japan, and Turkey. It was a market research question, an operations question, a clinical education question, and a procurement question, all at once. The research programme had to carry all of them.

My Role

I led the programme end-to-end: designing the study, building the discussion guides and stimuli, coordinating multilingual fieldwork across eight markets, driving synthesis, and owning the final strategic recommendations through to client presentation.

More than market landscaping — connecting clinical behaviour, operational feasibility, procurement logic, and adoption strategy into a single coherent view of global readiness.

Research Design

The programme ran for seven months across four sequential stages.

Literature review. We began with a landscape assessment to ground the team in current tools, anatomical considerations, anaesthesia practices, and procedural norms.

Quantitative survey. Surveying surgeons across eight geographies, mapping tool selection, imaging modalities, anaesthesia practices, procedural and anatomical considerations, and the limitations and mitigations surgeons employ — to understand current practices and the opportunities for addressing unmet latent needs.

In-depth interviews. In-depth interviews with proceduralists and purchasing decision-makers — conducted in local language across the eight geographies. Where the survey mapped what existed, the interviews explored why: how tool choices happen, how procurement works, where real-world workflow friction appears, and what clinicians actually do when the ideal tools are not available.

Stimuli-based protocol evaluation. Participants reacted to a proposed clinical workflow, designed to evaluate the feasibility of a proposed interventional protocol before clinical trial — surfacing what was workable, what needed modification, and where the proposed approach would collide with the realities of existing clinical environments.

What We Found

Five insights shaped the final adoption strategy.

1. The access route framework was globally validated

Interviewed physicians confirmed that the proposed access-route terminology matched their training and mental model. This gave the client confidence that the framework could travel globally and be carried directly into physician-facing materials and clinical trial documentation without significant rework.

2. Needle choice followed a decision architecture, not a preference

Needle choice was not simply a matter of what clinicians preferred. The synthesis revealed a consistent decision sequence: access route first, then visualisation device, then needle characteristics. Secondary drivers — training, familiarity, cost, reimbursement, availability, and institutional purchasing behaviour — operated within that architecture.

This distinction mattered for the adoption strategy. A campaign aimed at needle preference would miss the point. A strategy aligned with how decisions actually sequence had a realistic chance of changing practice.

3. Availability creates a self-reinforcing loop

The survey suggested limited global constraints around stock and availability. The interviews told a different story: proceduralists often adapt to what is already stocked. If a procedure can be completed with a narrow toolset, management has little incentive to introduce more specialised alternatives — which in turn limits clinician choice and slows the evolution of practice.

This was one of the most strategically important findings. It connected clinician behaviour, procurement logic, and market adoption barriers into a single mechanism — and pointed toward the intervention that would actually break the cycle: getting specialised tools into the hands of key opinion leaders who could demonstrate clinical value and generate pull from within the system.

4. Ultrasound is the path of least resistance — but training is the bottleneck

Clinicians preferred ultrasound for this procedure because it offers real-time visualisation, portability, no radiation exposure, lower logistical burden, and easier intra-operative use — making it the natural default across markets.

But adoption was not simply a function of availability. Protected training time, hands-on exposure, and sufficient case volume to build confidence were consistently identified as the barriers that mattered. The adoption problem was not access to technology — it was capability-building, workflow integration, and the readiness of clinical teams to use what they already had.

5. The protocol was feasible — but adoption would fail or succeed through operations

The proposed protocol was broadly described as familiar, low-disruption, and achievable within existing workflows — a meaningful validation of the client's core premise. Where friction appeared, it was logistical rather than clinical: the procedure itself was not the problem. The system that would have to support it was.

Impact

The research gave the client a clearer view of global readiness for a complex interventional procedure at an early and critical stage.

Specifically, it:

  • Established a global baseline of current tools, practices, and constraints across eight markets
  • Validated the access-route framework with clinicians across all eight geographies
  • Confirmed protocol feasibility across the proposed clinical workflow
  • Identified the operational refinements needed for rollout — CT logistics, anaesthesia language, imaging training pathways, procurement engagement — that were not visible through survey data alone
  • Translated the mixed-methods evidence into practical recommendations across clinical rollout, market access, procurement, and future research priorities

The client requested an expanded readout to a larger internal audience, and subsequently asked to scope the same research framework for an additional anatomy — a direct indicator of confidence in the programme's value.

What I Carry Forward

The survey expansion mistake is the finding from this project I return to most often in subsequent research design conversations.

I pushed the quantitative instrument toward qualitative nuance because I didn't want the output to become a shallow tool inventory. As scope expanded across multiple anatomies and procedure types, the questionnaire grew — open text fields, attitudinal scales, contextual sub-questions. The reasoning was understandable: surveys reach a larger sample than interviews, and there was so much we wanted to understand.

The result was a quantitative instrument that had absorbed qualitative ambitions. Respondents were asked to express nuanced clinical reasoning in a format that constrains and flattens precisely those responses. The data became difficult to analyse: too dense for clean quantitative treatment, too thin for genuine qualitative depth. In hindsight, I should have pushed earlier to constrain the survey scope, separate exploratory and quantitative objectives more cleanly, and protect the team from unnecessary analytical burden.

The principle I extracted — and carry into every mixed-methods design since — is this: each method should own something distinct. The survey's job was to measure prevalence and pattern. The interview's job was to explain and contextualise. The moment the survey tried to do the interview's job, it stopped doing its own well, and created analytical noise rather than triangulation signal.

Scope expansion before fieldwork is a synthesis problem in disguise. The time to catch it is before the first question is written.
