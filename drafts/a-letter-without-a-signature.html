The Challenge: Reframing the Question

A global healthcare client came to us with a question about a product. Their therapy involved a complex, hazardous treatment — a drug with significant risks for those who handle it repeatedly, over time, in real clinical and pharmacy environments. They wanted to understand how the product was being used and where the product design could be improved.

The research showed, fairly quickly, that the product was not the primary problem.

What we found was a service system — spanning pharmacy preparation, clinical administration, disposal, and staff safety management — that had developed differently across markets, with inconsistent protocols, unclear accountability, and a significant gap between what the official safety guidelines said and what actually happened at the bench.

The question shifted: not "how should the product change?" but "what does a safe, workable service system actually look like — and what is preventing it?"

The Approach

We conducted N=40 in-depth interviews across six countries, with pharmacists, pharmacy technicians, and environmental safety leads. The multi-market scope was deliberate: hazardous therapy handling is shaped heavily by local regulation, institutional culture, and resource constraints. What "safety" means in practice in Germany is not what it means in Brazil or South Korea.

Each interview was structured around a workflow mapping exercise — walking participants through the full handling process step by step, from receipt through preparation, administration, and disposal. This method consistently surfaces the gap between the formal protocol and the lived practice: the shortcuts people take not because they are careless, but because the system as designed does not fit the work as it exists.

What the Workflow Maps Showed

Three strategic insights emerged across the six markets with enough consistency to constitute systemic findings rather than local variation:

1. Compliance as performance, not protection. In multiple sites, staff followed visible safety protocols — gowning, documentation, disposal — while skipping less visible ones during high-pressure periods. Safety behaviour was shaped more by what could be observed and audited than by what was actually protective.

2. The knowledge gap between prescription and bench. Pharmacists who prepared the therapy knew significantly more about its risks than the clinical staff who administered it. The safety information did not travel with the drug. Downstream staff were exposed without context.

3. Accountability lived nowhere. In most sites, no single role owned the full safety chain. Environmental safety, pharmacy, and clinical teams each managed their segment. The transitions between segments — where the real risk concentrated — were unowned.

The Moment That Stayed With Me

Midway through fieldwork, I received an email from a participant who had completed their interview the week before. They had not wanted to say something on record. The email had no name — they had used a generic address.

They wrote about reproductive health. About being a younger female pharmacy technician, handling the therapy regularly, and not knowing what the long-term exposure meant for the future they were planning. They had asked their manager. The manager had referred them to the product safety sheet. The product safety sheet addressed acute exposure, not chronic low-level handling over years. There was no answer. There was nowhere to get one.

This was not an outlier. What the email surfaced was that "safety" as the client understood it — measured in compliance rates and protocol adherence — was not the same as safety as the people handling this therapy experienced it. One was about institutional liability. The other was about trust: whether the system they worked within was actually looking out for them.

The anonymous letter became one of the most important pieces of data in the project. It clarified what was at stake in a way no chart could.

Outcome and Strategic Recommendations

The research was delivered as a service system analysis, not a product evaluation. Recommendations spanned:

  • A cross-functional safety accountability model to close the ownership gaps between pharmacy, clinical, and environmental safety
  • A knowledge transfer protocol to ensure safety information travelled with the therapy through the full handling chain
  • Market-specific implementation priorities based on the workflow gap analysis
  • A recommendation to initiate ongoing dialogue with frontline handlers — not just about protocol compliance, but about trust

The client described the reframe as unexpected and initially uncomfortable, and ultimately as the most useful output they had received on this product. The human dimension of safety — not just whether staff were following the rules, but whether they felt the system was protecting them — entered the internal conversation in a way it had not before.
