Each Method Should Own Something

N=40 survey, N=24 IDIs, literature review, stimuli testing — a study designed around triangulation that taught me the most important thing about mixed methods: discipline.

Role

Lead Researcher

Timeline

Multi-phase, ~10 months

Methods

Outcome

Delivered an adoption strategy for ITDD clinical rollout, with clear triangulated findings and a hard-won principle about research design.

The Challenge

A global medical device client was preparing for the clinical rollout of an ITDD (Intratumoral Drug Delivery) approach in head and neck oncology. They needed to understand the conditions for adoption — what would make clinicians use it, what would slow or prevent uptake, and what the roadmap to widespread adoption looked like across markets.

The research design called for triangulation: a quantitative survey to establish prevalence and pattern across a large sample, qualitative in-depth interviews to understand the underlying reasons and context, a literature review to map the existing evidence landscape, and stimuli testing to evaluate clinician responses to the proposed approach.

Eight geographies. Four methods. One study. On paper, it was a comprehensive research design. In practice, it became an object lesson in what can go wrong when methods are not given clear, distinct mandates.

Research Design: The Triangulation Argument

The case for mixed methods in clinical adoption research is sound. Survey data can tell you how widespread a behaviour or belief is; interviews can tell you why; literature can tell you what the evidence base supports; stimuli testing can tell you how clinicians respond when the concept is made concrete. Each method answers a question the others cannot.

The study was designed with this logic. A quantitative survey with N=40 clinicians across eight geographies would establish the landscape: current practice patterns, familiarity with ITDD approaches, perceived barriers and enablers. Interviews with N=24 clinicians would go deeper into the reasoning, the clinical context, the unspoken concerns that survey scales cannot reach.

This is the logic I started with. What I learned is that logic is not enough — you need discipline in execution.

The Mistake: When the Survey Tried to Be an Interview

Midway through the survey design phase, the scope expanded. The questionnaire grew to capture more nuance: open-text fields were added, attitudinal scales were developed with multiple sub-items, questions about context and edge cases multiplied. The reasoning was understandable — there was so much we wanted to know, and the survey reached a larger sample than the interviews would.

The result was a quantitative instrument that had absorbed qualitative ambitions. Respondents were being asked to express nuanced clinical reasoning in survey format — a medium that constrains and flattens precisely the kinds of response we were trying to capture. The data became difficult to analyse: too dense for clean quantitative treatment, too thin for genuine qualitative depth. N=40 people had told us something, but it was harder to say what.

The principle I extracted from this — and carry into every mixed-methods design since — is that each method should own something distinct. The survey's job was to measure prevalence and pattern. The interview's job was to explain and contextualise. The moment the survey tried to do the interview's job, it stopped doing its own well, and created analytical noise rather than triangulation signal.

I revised the survey instrument late in the design phase, cutting it back to what quantitative data can legitimately carry. It was a late correction, and it cost time. But the integrity of the triangulation depended on it.

Key Findings

Across the eight geographies, three insights shaped the adoption strategy:

The availability loop. In most markets, access to ITDD was limited to academic and specialist centres. Clinicians at community and regional hospitals — where the majority of head and neck oncology patients were treated — had no exposure to the approach and no realistic pathway to adoption. The adoption strategy had to address the ecosystem, not just the individual clinician.

The ultrasound bottleneck. Image guidance for ITDD procedures depended on ultrasound capability that was not uniformly available or consistently used across markets. In several geographies, the practical barrier to adoption was not clinician acceptance but equipment access and training — a finding the survey prevalence data helped quantify and the interviews gave texture to.

Protocol feasibility, not efficacy. Clinician hesitation about ITDD was less about doubt in the underlying evidence than about uncertainty around what a standardised, workable protocol looked like in their specific setting. The literature review was critical here: it allowed us to map what existed versus what was missing, and gave the adoption strategy a clear gap to address.

Stimuli Testing

The stimuli testing phase evaluated clinician responses to a proposed protocol framework — a structured approach to ITDD integration into existing head and neck oncology workflows. Sessions were designed to surface what clinicians found workable, what they would modify, and where the proposed approach collided with the realities of their setting.

This phase produced the most actionable output: a direct link between the research findings and a concrete implementation artefact, with clinician feedback shaping the final protocol recommendations.

Strategic Recommendations & Impact

The study delivered an adoption strategy for ITDD clinical rollout structured around:

  • A tiered rollout model prioritising specialist centres as proof-of-concept sites before broader dissemination
  • A protocol standardisation roadmap addressing the feasibility gap identified in the stimuli testing
  • Market-specific barrier analyses for each of the eight geographies
  • Recommendations for the evidence gap that the literature review had identified, informing future research investment

The client used the triangulated findings to brief their clinical education and market access teams. The availability loop insight, in particular, shifted the framing of the rollout strategy from clinician adoption to ecosystem development.

What I Carry Forward

The survey expansion mistake is the finding from this project I return to most often in subsequent research design conversations. Mixed-methods studies are not simply more rigorous than single-method studies — they require more discipline, because the temptation to blur the boundaries between methods grows with the ambition of the design.

The rule is simple: before adding a question to a survey, ask what method owns that question. If the answer is "the interview," take it out of the survey. If a method cannot answer a question distinctly, adding the question makes the analysis harder, not richer.
