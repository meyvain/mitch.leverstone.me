The Isolation Problem

This research aimed to shape the next-generation cardiac mapping system for a major player in the medtech field. The client came with a clear constraint: focus on the software. Hardware, they told us, was settled.

This is what I came to call the Isolation Problem. When a client comes to you with a defined scope, your role is to work within it. And then you begin, and you start to realise how much more complex the problem is. How do you isolate your findings within a deeply complex, systematic network of intertwined hardware, software, and human components — the very foundation of the user's true experience and use environment?

You can't. Not fully. And doing so honestly required us to name that tension from the start.

Phase 0: Into the Operating Theatre

"You can't design for reality without first stepping into it."

Before interviews, before workshops, we went to the operating theatre. We observed live minimal invasive heart surgeries across North America, watching both our client's system and key competitors being used in real procedures.

"Ethnography doesn't just answer questions — it reveals the ones you didn't know to ask."

I was observing a cardiac ablation in Canada. The surgeon was navigating their catheter through the anatomy of the patient's heart — a humbling thing to witness. As the procedure progressed and the team grew confident, I saw an opportunity. With their permission, I asked a few questions.

I turned their attention to the screen. A large flat monitor positioned in front of them displaying a complex array of data: live imaging, a previous scan for reference, various system readings. The way it was laid out was, objectively, the least comfortable configuration for the surgeon to see the live imaging they were using to navigate. They were hyper-focused on it — and hadn't noticed.

I, coming from outside, unburdened by clinical responsibility in that moment, was able to see it clearly. I asked, "Maybe I'm missing something here, but could this be moved in front of you?" They looked at the screen, then turned toward the control room: "Can we change the setting here? Can you move the other panels and show me just this, right in front?"

A window into the obvious. Hidden information that would never surface in an interview or survey — because you can't ask a question you don't yet know to ask. That is the unique value of being in the room.

Observation also allows you to see beyond the device itself. In the operating theatre, you don't only witness how the system is used — you see the full backstage: how many hands a device passes through before the primary user touches it, who places it and where, the cascade of events and people that precede the critical moment. The complexity of that environment was, in itself, a finding.

Making the Client Feel It

Later in the project, I brought key client stakeholders with me into a hospital setting in London. For some of them, it was the first time they had seen their product used in real life, or spoken directly with the people who use it every day.

They were confronted with the complexity of the workflow implications of their upcoming initiatives. They heard clinical staff speaking candidly — and candidly critically — about a product they had been working on for years, without knowing these stakeholders were in the room. They received a vivid, lived experience rather than a written report.

What would make the greater impact? What would last?

Mapping the Ecosystem: N=12 Interviews

With field observations grounding our understanding, we moved into in-depth interviews with N=12 cardiac mappers — across varying backgrounds, experience levels, and shift patterns, including both day and night coverage. The goal was to build a rich understanding of the key pain points and map the user workflow from end to end.

Synthesis was built around the key areas we had agreed with the client: most-used features, competitive wish lists, edge cases encountered, unmet needs, and open "magic wand" questions. These were then distilled into user needs statements, which would directly feed the next phase.

We also incorporated a simulator day, where different cardiac mapping cases were demonstrated on a software training simulator used by cardiac mapping technicians. This gave us a further layer of workflow data in a lower-stakes, more accessible environment than the OR.

The result was a user journey map with focused call-outs on the highest-friction and most communication-intensive points across the workflow — a clear picture of where the software was doing its job and where it was not.

The Workshop: 20 Stakeholders, Two Days

With the problem space mapped and user needs synthesised, we hosted a two-day ideation workshop at the client's headquarters. Approximately 20 key stakeholders flew in — spanning clinical, engineering, marketing, strategy, and regulatory functions.

The session moved through our key findings from the field and interview phases, the workflow, and structured activities on user needs prioritisation and strategic fit alignment. The goal was not to present and debrief — it was to reach shared clarity on what mattered most and why, and to begin generating the ideas that would carry us into the next phase.

Three concept directions emerged from the two days as the most promising candidates for further testing.

Crafting the Stimuli

The workshop generated strong but raw ideas. My role was to translate these into testable stimuli for the concept testing phase — defining clear software feature directions and their specific implications on clinical workflow, and transforming abstract possibilities into concrete, testable scenarios.

This phase was a genuine cross-disciplinary undertaking. The stimuli were crafted collaboratively between myself as research lead, a UI designer, and AI tools. It taught me a great deal about cross-disciplinary communication: how to act on research learnings in partnership with a designer, developing visual stimuli for a product that exists, at that stage, as little more than a title and an intent.

And it is where I made a costly mistake.

The stimuli I delivered were too bland. Too generic. I had focused on the "how" of producing visual artefacts without first solving the harder problem: defining clearly what we needed to communicate. The UI had no real meaning — it didn't push participants beyond what they already knew from the existing system.

The client's feedback was direct and entirely appropriate: "This doesn't really communicate anything to the participant beyond what we already have in the system."

Mortifying, in the moment. But the lesson was important and lasting. The researcher's job in a stimulus design process is not to support the visual execution — it is to define the problem space with enough precision that the designer has something real to build from. Working from ambiguous needs to develop genuine, testable meaning was a first for me on this project, and it was a difficult and worthwhile introduction to the second diamond — the space where discovery meets design.

Concept Testing: From Resonance to Roadmap

With revised stimuli in hand, we moved into a multi-round concept testing phase.

Round 1 — Resonance Testing: We evaluated the three concept directions with both Electrophysiologists (EPs) and cardiac mappers to understand which specific features and elements had the strongest potential to positively influence workflow, perception, and adoption. We were looking not just for what participants liked, but what would genuinely change how they worked.

Round 2 — Prioritisation: A final round with cardiac mappers, focused on ranking the key software improvements and identifying competitive differentiation potential. This phase moved the project from validated ideas to a clear development sequence.

The Roadmap — and the Recommendation Beyond It

The output was a prioritised, evidence-based software development roadmap and a structured product pipeline for the client's engineering teams — grounded entirely in what we had seen, heard, and tested across three phases of research.

The ethnographic phase did not open the door to a formal hardware conversation — the client's scope remained on software. But what it gave us was something equally valuable: a genuine understanding of the full system the software had to function within. We wrote our recommendations accordingly, including a clear call for the business to invest in understanding the relationship between software, hardware, usability, human experience, and adoption as an integrated whole.

Whether that door opens is not within our control. But we put the recommendation on record, grounded in evidence, and hoped it would be a contributing voice in that conversation.

"Ethnography is where empathy meets strategy, shaping solutions that truly matter."
